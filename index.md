## Statement 

> Our target learning audience is NYU students, especially those engaging with the Wasserman Center as they prepare for internships and full-time roles. A consistent learning need we see (and have experienced) is building self-confidence and clear, repeatable communication practices for networking and professional outreach. Many students understand they should prepare, but don’t know how to prepare in a structured way, so anxiety takes over and conversations feel awkward, inefficient, or hard to sustain. This shows up in a few key skill gaps such as managing networking anxiety through low-pressure practice, initiating and maintaining professional conversations, asking thoughtful questions, explaining why they’re interested in a role or industry, and writing clear, structured networking messages (with templates they can adapt). 

> We imagine AI can directly support this need by acting as a personalized, always-available coaching partner: it can simulate networking scenarios, provide feedback on tone and clarity, help students draft and refine outreach messages, and offer practice prompts and answer templates that build confidence over time. Because AI practice is private and “no-stakes,” it can lower the barrier to repetition, while also improving accessibility for students who may be less comfortable speaking English as a first language and benefit from individualized scaffolding and language support.

## 1.1 Problem Statement
**Team name:** Lumo 

**Project Title:** AI-Powered Research Paper Summarization Through Poetry

**Target Learning Audience:**
Undergraduate and graduate students, early-career researchers, and lifelong learners who struggle with engaging with dense academic texts.

**Identified Learning Need:**<br>
Reading and comprehending research papers is a significant challenge for many students and researchers, particularly those new to academic discourse. Studies indicate that academic writing is often dense, filled with jargon, and difficult to digest, leading to cognitive overload and disengagement (Swales & Feak, 2012). Additionally, students from non-English-speaking backgrounds or those unfamiliar with specific disciplinary conventions may find it even harder to extract key insights from research articles (Flowerdew, 2019). Students often express frustration with parsing long, complex papers, full of academic jargon, which in turn hinders their ability to effectively engage in academic discussions.

**Rationale for AI Assistance:**<br>
AI can transform the way students interact with academic literature by converting complex research papers into culturally resonant poetry, making key insights more engaging and memorable. Poetry, especially when adapted to different cultural styles, enhances comprehension through rhythm, metaphor, and storytelling, which can make abstract concepts more accessible. Prior work has utilized art as a medium to make academic research more engaging (Edens and Potter, 2001), but poetry has not been frequently utilized. Poetry is also an interesting artistic medium to convert academic text to different cultural contexts. AI-powered natural language processing can analyze research papers, extract essential themes, and creatively reframe them in poetic forms, offering students a personalized artistic way to process academic content. By leveraging AI to bridge the gap between dense research and creative expression, this tool can foster deeper engagement in academic research, and make academic written content more accessible to students from diverse backgrounds. 

**References:** Swales, J. M., & Feak, C. B. (2004). *Academic writing for graduate students: Essential tasks and skills* (Vol. 1). Ann Arbor, MI: University of Michigan Press.  
Flowerdew, J. (2019). The linguistic disadvantage of scholars who write in English as an additional language: Myth or reality. *Language Teaching*, 52(2), 249-260.  
Edens, K. M., & Potter, E. F. (2001). Promoting conceptual understanding through pictorial representation. *Studies in Art Education*, 42(3), 214-233.

## 1.2 Needfinding
**Original Problem Statement** <br>
NYU students preparing for internships and jobs need structured, low-pressure ways to practice and build confidence in networking and professional communication.


**Context Description**<br>
***Our users*** are NYU students in their final year who are actively preparing for internships and full-time job applications. These students come from diverse academic and language backgrounds, and many are navigating professional networking and interviewing for the first time.<br>

***The problem*** typically occurs during the job search and preparation period. Preparation can take place in self-directed settings,such as at home, in dorms, or between classes. While resources like the Wasserman Center exist, students may not always be able to receive individualized support due to time constraints or availability.

***The main activity*** being performed is interview and networking preparation. This includes researching company background, understanding role expectations, practicing responses to common interview questions, developing a strong self-introduction, and preparing for professional conversations. Students want to refine how they communicate their interests and experiences clearly and confidently. 


**Needfinding Method** <br>
We conducted brief interviews (10–20 minutes each) consisting of seven questions. Our main participants were NYU undergraduate and graduate students who are currently in the job preparation phase.
Our questions were designed to explore how students currently approach networking and where they struggle, especially in terms of confidence and communication. We focused on understanding their preparation process, emotional experiences (such as anxiety), and what strategies or tools they rely on. We also looked for patterns in successful vs. difficult interactions to identify opportunities for structured support.

**Key Findings**
1. Students experience high pre-conversation anxiety
Students often feel nervous before networking, especially due to language concerns and fear of sounding unprofessional.Anxiety typically decreases once the conversation starts.
One participant shared: “I was worried my English wouldn’t sound professional enough.”

2. Networking norms are unclear
Students are unsure about:How direct he/she should be,when and how to follow up and what questions are appropriate. This reflects a gap in understanding social expectations, not just skills.

3. Preparation is informal and inconsistent
Students prepare themselves by researching people/companies, practicing short introductions and preparing a few questions. However, there is no clear or structured approach.

4. AI is used for practice, not memorization
AI tools like chatgpt are mostly used for simulating conversations or organizing responses. However students want to avoid memorizing scripts and prefer some more flexible practice.

5. Students prefer low-risk networking
Students often start networking with alumni, classmates and personal connections.Cold outreach is often considered scary due to low confidence.

6. Confidence is the main barrier
Students generally know how to prepare, but struggle to speak clearly and present themselves confidently. Self-doubt and comparison with others increase this gap.

7. Natural conversations lead to better outcomes
Positive experiences happen when interactions feel conversational, low-pressure and mutually engaging. However there are no current tools that can stimulate or achieve this setting.

**Refined Understanding of the Problem** <br>
Our original problem statement still holds but needs some refinement. While students do need low-pressure ways to practice networking, our findings show that the core issue is not just lack of practice, but a gap between knowing how to prepare and being able to perform confidently in real conversations.

Students are using strategies such as research, short introductions, and tools like AI, but these efforts are unstructured and lack feedback. Additionally, many students, especially international students, struggle with unclear social norms and language-related anxiety, which further reduces confidence.

## 1.3 Literature Review

**Problem Context**<br>
The project investigates the challenge that International, undergraduate, and graduate students often face significant anxiety and a lack of preparation when entering professional networking situations. 

Needfinding suggested that early job seekers frequently lack exposure to real-world professional communication norms and may struggle with cultural nuances in professional settings. Consequently, there is a clear need for a low-pressure, accessible, and repeatable environment where students can practice and build confidence in their communication skills.

The goal of this literature review is to explore technologies that support social skills for students.

**Source Reviewed**<br>
Research on using general AI tools explores how platforms like ChatGPT and Google Interview Warmup can simulate interview scenarios to help job seekers build confidence (The Muse Editors, 2024). These tools allow users to rehearse common questions and receive initial feedback on their delivery in a low-pressure environment.

Research on specialized systems like CONVERSATE introduces "dialogic feedback," which enables a two-way interactive dialogue between the student and the AI (Daryanto et al., 2024). This system uses Large Language Models (LLMs) to ground feedback in professional frameworks like the STAR method while encouraging students to reflect on their own performance through AI-assisted annotation.

Another approach involves using multi-agent AI simulations to scale experiential learning for soft skills such as business pitching (Mollick et al., 2024). These simulations, such as PitchQuest, utilize AI role-players, mentors, and evaluators to provide students with repeatable practice opportunities that would otherwise be difficult or expensive to provide with human.

Finally, systematic reviews of AI-driven interviewing systems highlight a transition toward multimodal feedback and immersive avatars but identify a lack of pedagogical scaffolding and emotional sensitivity in current tools (Sanjana, Li, & He, 2025). These reviews emphasize the need for more inclusive, learner-centered designs that can support broader professional communication beyond narrow interview formats.

**Key Ideas from the Literature** <br>
Across these sources, several central technological and pedagogical approaches have emerged:

First, many systems rely on multiple AI agents to simulate various professional roles, such as recruiters, alumni or investors, to create realistic social scenarios.

Second, AI can help provide a safe, repeatable, low-stakes, and low-cost environment for students to practice certain skills, such as interviews, group discussions, business idea pitches, and other technical or professional skills, before they engage in real high-stakes tasks.

Third, most AI systems provide users with formative feedback, offering refined responses along with specific examples for improvement. This feedback and the accompanying examples are usually grounded in established professional frameworks to help students structure their answers.

**Existing Solutions and Their Limitations** <br>
Despite the advancements in AI communication tools, several critical limitations persist across existing solutions. 

Many current tools are described as fragmented, focusing narrowly on technical interview questions while failing to support the broader spectrum of professional communication, such as networking and small talk. 

Furthermore, AI-generated feedback often lacks the social and cultural nuance required to guide international students through the complex norms of real-world human interaction. 

There is also a notable absence of emotional sensitivity in many platforms, which can lead to generic advice that does not address the specific anxiety or background of the learner. 

Finally, while these systems are scalable, many struggle to provide the deeply personalized coaching and alignment with real learner needs that traditional human mentors offer.

**Implications for Your Project** <br>
The literature suggests that our project has a significant opportunity to move beyond standard interview preparation by focusing on realistic networking scenarios, including elevator pitches and professional small talk. 

By utilizing multiple AI personas to simulate different networking contexts, the project can help students build confidence across a variety of social interactions. Implementing a dialogic feedback model will allow students to act as active agents in their learning, using the AI as an interactive coach rather than just a testing tool. Ultimately, designing for inclusivity by addressing cultural and linguistic barriers in networking scenarios should be taken into account.
